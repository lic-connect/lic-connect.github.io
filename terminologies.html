LEARN THE INSURANCE BASICS

A

Accidental Death Benefit

An add-on (rider) to a life insurance policy that pays an additional sum to the beneficiary if the insured dies as a result of an accident.

Example: Rohan buys a ₹50 lakh life insurance policy and adds a ₹25 lakh Accidental Death Benefit rider. If he dies in a car accident, his family receives ₹75 lakh. If he dies from a heart attack, they receive ₹50 lakh.

Accumulation Period

The phase of an annuity or cash value life insurance policy during which the policyholder pays premiums and the account value grows, before the payout (annuitization) phase begins.

Example: Sarah, age 40, buys a deferred annuity. The 20 years she spends paying premiums into the annuity before she retires at age 60 is the accumulation period.

Accumulation Value

The total value in a policy's cash value account, including all premiums paid and interest or investment gains credited, before any surrender charges are deducted.

Example: After 10 years, David's universal life policy shows an accumulation value of ₹2 lakh. If he were to surrender it, he would receive this amount minus any applicable surrender fees.

Actuarial Assumptions

Predictions made by actuaries regarding future events affecting an insurance policy, such as mortality rates, interest earnings, and policy persistency, used to calculate premiums and reserves.

Example: To price a new policy, an insurer's actuaries assume a 5% average annual return on investments and use a specific mortality table to predict how many policyholders will pass away each year.

Actuarial Equity

The principle that policyholders with similar risk characteristics should be charged similar premiums for the same coverage.

Example: Two 35-year-old healthy, non-smoking males applying for the same life insurance policy should be offered identical premium rates due to the principle of actuarial equity.

Actuarial Table

A statistical chart showing probabilities of life events, such as a mortality table which shows the probability of death at each age.

Example: An insurance company refers to its actuarial table (specifically, a mortality table) to determine that a 40-year-old male has a 0.2% chance of dying in the next year, which helps them calculate the cost of his insurance.

Actuary

A professional who uses mathematical and statistical models to analyze the financial costs of risk and uncertainty, primarily for pricing insurance policies and setting reserves.

Example: When LIC launches a new plan, an actuary is responsible for calculating how much the premium should be to ensure the company can cover all future claims and expenses.

Adjusted Premium

A premium that has been modified from the standard rate to account for special circumstances, such as a substandard risk rating or the addition of riders.

Example: The standard premium for a policy is ₹10,000. Because the applicant is a professional stunt driver, the insurer adds a ₹2,000 surcharge, making the adjusted premium ₹12,000 per year.

Admitted Assets

Assets of an insurance company that are permitted by state law to be included in the company's financial statements for determining its solvency.

Example: An insurer's portfolio of government bonds and blue-chip stocks are considered admitted assets, while an old, unappraised office building might be considered a non-admitted asset by regulators.

Agent

A licensed individual who sells and services insurance policies on behalf of an insurance company.

Example: Sunita meets with Mr. Verma, an LIC agent, who explains the benefits of different plans and helps her fill out the application form for a new policy.

Aggregate Limit

The maximum total amount an insurance company will pay for all covered losses during a specified policy period.

Example: A small group health policy might have an aggregate limit of ₹1 crore per year, meaning the insurer will not pay more than that total amount for all claims from all members of the group in that year.

Aggregate Mortality Table

A mortality table based on the combined death rates of a large group of people, without differentiation for specific risk factors other than age and sex.

Example: A national statistics office might publish an aggregate mortality table for the entire country's population, which actuaries can use as a baseline before creating more specific tables for insured lives.

Alternative Settlement Option

Any method of receiving policy proceeds other than a single lump-sum payment, such as receiving payments over a fixed period or for life.

Example: Instead of taking a ₹1 crore death benefit as a lump sum, the beneficiary chooses an alternative settlement option to receive ₹1 lakh every month for the next 10 years.

Annual Renewable Term

A type of term life insurance that guarantees the policyholder the right to renew the policy each year without evidence of insurability, though the premium may increase annually with age.

Example: Ken buys an Annual Renewable Term policy. His premium is low in the first year, but it increases every year on his policy anniversary as he gets older. He doesn't need to take a new medical exam to continue the cover.

Annual Statement

A detailed financial report that an insurance company must file with the state insurance department each year, showing its assets, liabilities, and overall financial condition.

Example: The insurance regulator, IRDAI, reviews LIC's Annual Statement to ensure the company has enough funds and is financially stable enough to pay all its policyholders' future claims.

Annuitant

The person, usually the policy owner, who receives the periodic income payments from an annuity contract.

Example: Mrs. Kapoor buys a pension plan from LIC. When she retires and starts receiving her monthly pension, she becomes the annuitant.

Annuity

A financial product that pays out a fixed stream of payments to an individual, primarily used as an income stream for retirees.

Example: Raj invests a lump sum of ₹50 lakh into an immediate annuity plan. The insurance company then starts paying him a guaranteed pension of ₹30,000 every month for the rest of his life.

Application

The form filled out by an individual seeking insurance, providing personal information and medical history that the insurer uses to underwrite the policy.

Example: To get a life insurance policy, Aisha must complete a 10-page application where she answers questions about her health, occupation, and lifestyle.

Assignee

The person or entity (e.g., a bank) to whom the rights of a life insurance policy are transferred by the policy owner (the assignor).

Example: When Ajay takes a home loan, the bank becomes the assignee of his life insurance policy. If Ajay defaults, the bank can claim the policy's value.

Assignment

The legal transfer of a policyholder's rights, title, and interest in a policy to another person or entity.

Example: Priya needs a business loan. The bank agrees, but requires collateral. Priya completes a formal Assignment of her life insurance policy to the bank, giving them rights to the policy's value until the loan is repaid.

Assignor

The policy owner who transfers their rights in a life insurance policy to an assignee.

Example: In the loan scenario, Priya is the policy owner who transfers her rights, making her the assignor. The bank, which receives the rights, is the assignee.

Automatic Premium Loan

A provision in a cash value policy that allows the insurer to automatically borrow from the policy's cash value to pay a premium that is past due, preventing the policy from lapsing.

Example: Meena forgets to pay her ₹5,000 quarterly premium. Instead of the policy lapsing, the insurance company automatically takes a ₹5,000 loan from her policy's cash value to cover the payment, keeping the policy active.

Automatic Reinstatement

A policy provision that allows for the automatic restoration of coverage under certain conditions after a claim has been paid, often seen with specific riders.

Example: A critical illness rider pays out on a first heart attack. An automatic reinstatement provision might allow the rider to become active again after one year, offering coverage for a different future illness.

Aviation Exclusion

A policy clause that excludes coverage for death occurring while flying in a non-commercial aircraft.

Example: Tom is a hobbyist pilot. His life insurance policy has an aviation exclusion, meaning if he dies while flying his private plane, his family will not receive the death benefit.

Avoidance

The act of an insurer declaring a policy void from its inception, usually due to material misrepresentation or concealment by the applicant.

Example: If an applicant fails to disclose a recent cancer diagnosis and the insurer finds out, the insurer can execute an avoidance of the contract, treating it as if it never existed.

B

Backdating

The practice of making a policy effective on a date prior to the application date, often to secure a lower premium based on a younger age.

Example: Amit's birthday was on May 10th, which moved him into a higher age bracket for premiums. On May 25th, he applies for a policy and requests to backdate it to May 1st, allowing him to get the lower premium rate of his previous age.

Beneficiary

The person, people, trust, or institution designated to receive the death benefit from a life insurance policy.

Example: When Ravi buys a life insurance policy, he names his wife, Priya, as the beneficiary. If Ravi passes away, Priya will receive the policy money.

Beneficiary Clause

The section of the insurance policy that specifies who will receive the policy proceeds.

Example: In Maria's policy document, the Beneficiary Clause clearly states: "Primary Beneficiary: John D'Souza (Spouse), Contingent Beneficiary: Anna D'Souza (Daughter)."

Binding Agreement

A temporary insurance agreement that provides coverage from the date of application until the policy is officially issued or declined.

Example: When an agent accepts the first premium, they might issue a binding agreement, meaning the applicant is insured from that moment, even if the formal policy document takes weeks to arrive.

Binding Receipt

A receipt given for a premium payment that binds the insurance company to provide coverage starting from the date of the receipt, subject to the terms of the receipt.

Example: Ali pays his first premium on June 1st and gets a binding receipt. He tragically dies in an accident on June 5th. Even though the policy wasn't formally issued, the insurer must pay the claim because the binding receipt provided immediate coverage.

Blanket Insurance

A single policy that covers a group of people, such as employees of a company or members of an organization.

Example: A university purchases a blanket insurance policy to cover all its students for accidents that might occur on campus or during university-sponsored trips.

Bonus (Reversionary, Terminal)

A share of the insurer's profits paid to holders of participating policies. A reversionary bonus is declared annually and added to the sum assured. A terminal bonus is paid once, at maturity or death.

Example: Every year, Deepak gets a statement that a reversionary bonus of ₹20,000 has been added to his endowment policy. When the policy matures after 20 years, LIC pays a final, one-time terminal bonus of ₹50,000 on top of all the accumulated bonuses.

Book Reserve

The amount of money an insurance company sets aside as a liability in its financial books to cover future claims.

Example: For every active policy, an insurer's accounting department maintains a book reserve, which is the calculated amount they need to have invested to fulfill their future promise to pay the claim.

Broker

A licensed insurance professional who represents the client (the buyer) and can sell policies from multiple insurance companies.

Example: Instead of going directly to LIC, Fatima consults an insurance broker. The broker shows her and compares life insurance plans from LIC, HDFC Life, and ICICI Prudential, helping her choose the best fit.

Burial Insurance

A type of final expense insurance with a small death benefit, specifically intended to cover funeral and burial costs.

Example: An 80-year-old widow buys a ₹50,000 burial insurance policy so that her children won't have to worry about funeral expenses when she passes away.

Business Continuation Insurance

Insurance used to ensure a business can continue operating after the death or disability of a key owner or employee, often funding a buy-sell agreement.

Example: A successful restaurant is famous because of its head chef. The restaurant owners buy a business continuation insurance policy on the chef's life to provide funds to hire a replacement and cover losses if the chef were to die unexpectedly.

Buy-Sell Agreement

A legal contract that outlines how a deceased or departing partner's share of a business will be bought out by the remaining partners, often funded by life insurance.

Example: Two business partners, Sameer and Arjun, sign a buy-sell agreement. They each buy a life insurance policy on the other's life. If Sameer dies, the insurance payout gives Arjun the cash to buy Sameer's half of the business from his family.

C

Captive Agent

An insurance agent who works exclusively for one insurance company and sells only their policies.

Example: Mr. Sharma is a captive agent for LIC. This means he can only sell you LIC policies and cannot offer you a plan from Max Life or Bajaj Allianz.

Cash Surrender Value

The amount of money a policyholder receives if they voluntarily terminate a permanent life insurance policy before its maturity. It's the accumulated cash value minus any surrender charges.

Example: After paying premiums for 10 years, Geeta's policy has an accumulated cash value of ₹1,50,000 and a surrender charge of ₹10,000. If she cancels the policy, her cash surrender value is ₹1,40,000.

Cash Value Accumulation Test (CVAT)

One of two IRS tests a policy must pass to qualify as life insurance for tax purposes. It limits the cash value relative to the death benefit.

Example: The insurer designs a universal life policy ensuring that the cash value never exceeds a certain percentage of the death benefit at any given age, thereby passing the CVAT and retaining its tax advantages.

Catastrophic Loss

A severe loss that is very large and unexpected, such as from a natural disaster or pandemic, affecting many policyholders at once.

Example: A major earthquake in a city would be a catastrophic loss for an insurance company, as they would face a huge number of simultaneous claims for life, health, and property damage.

Ceding Company

The insurance company that transfers a portion of its risk to another insurer (the reinsurer) in a reinsurance agreement.

Example: ABC Insurance sells a massive ₹50 crore policy to a corporation. Since this is too much risk, ABC Insurance (the ceding company) pays a premium to XYZ Reinsurance to cover ₹30 crore of that risk.

Certificate of Insurance

A document issued to a member of a group insurance plan, providing evidence of coverage and summarizing the benefits.

Example: When a new employee joins a company, they don't get a full policy document for the group life cover. Instead, they receive a certificate of insurance confirming they are covered for ₹20 lakh.

Claim

A formal request by a beneficiary to an insurance company to pay the death benefit upon the death of the insured.

Example: After her husband's death, Mrs. Verma fills out the required forms, attaches the death certificate, and submits them to the insurance company. This process is filing a claim.

Clause

A specific section or provision within an insurance policy that details a particular condition, right, or exclusion.

Example: The "Suicide Clause" in a life insurance policy is a specific paragraph that explains the company's rules and payout conditions if the insured's death is due to suicide.

Co-insurance

A provision where the policyholder and the insurer share the cost of a covered loss in a specified ratio (e.g., 80/20). More common in health insurance than life insurance.

Example: Rina's health insurance has an 80/20 co-insurance clause. For a ₹1 lakh hospital bill, the insurer pays ₹80,000, and Rina pays the remaining ₹20,000.

Co-insured

An additional person covered under an insurance policy alongside the primary insured.

Example: On a joint life insurance policy, both the husband and wife are named as insured persons. The wife is a co-insured on the policy taken by the husband.

Collateral Assignment

The temporary transfer of a policy's cash value and/or death benefit to a lender as security for a loan. The assignment ends when the loan is repaid.

Example: To secure a business loan of ₹20 lakh, Vikram makes a collateral assignment of his ₹50 lakh life insurance policy to the bank. The bank's rights are limited to the loan amount, and the assignment is canceled once he repays the loan.

Commission

The fee paid by an insurance company to its agent or broker for selling a policy.

Example: When an agent sells a policy with a ₹25,000 annual premium, the insurance company pays them a percentage of that premium as their commission for the sale.

Commutation

The process of paying a future stream of income (like an annuity) as a single lump sum.

Example: At retirement, a policyholder is entitled to a pension of ₹10,000 per month for life. They choose to exercise commutation for one-third of it, receiving a single lump sum payment upfront and a reduced pension of ₹6,667 per month.

Commutation Function

Actuarial symbols used as a shorthand to simplify the calculation of premiums and reserves.

Example: Instead of writing a long, complex formula, an actuary uses a standard commutation function like 'Dx' or 'Nx' to represent a specific value in their calculations, which is understood by other actuaries.

Concealment

The intentional failure of an applicant to disclose a material fact to the insurer, which can lead to the policy being voided.

Example: On his insurance application, David doesn't mention that he is a regular smoker. This is concealment, and if he dies from a smoking-related illness, the insurer could refuse to pay the claim.

Conditional Receipt

A receipt given when the first premium is paid with the application. It makes coverage effective from that date, provided the applicant is found to be insurable as a standard risk.

Example: Anjali applies for a policy, pays the first premium, and gets a conditional receipt. If she is found to be healthy during underwriting, her coverage will be considered to have started from the date of the receipt, not the date the policy was finally approved.

Consideration

A fundamental element of a legal contract. For the policyholder, it's the premium payments and statements in the application. For the insurer, it's the promise to pay the benefit.

Example: In an insurance contract, the policyholder's consideration is the premium they agree to pay. The insurance company's consideration is its promise to pay the death benefit as per the contract terms.

Contestability Period

A limited period (usually the first two years of a policy) during which the insurer can investigate and dispute the validity of the policy, typically on grounds of material misrepresentation.

Example: If a person dies 18 months after buying a policy, the insurer can investigate their application for any lies. If they die after 3 years, the contestability period is over, and the insurer must pay the claim without investigation (unless fraud is proven).

Contingent Beneficiary

The person or entity designated to receive the death benefit if the primary beneficiary dies before the insured.

Example: Mohan names his wife as the primary beneficiary and his son as the contingent beneficiary. If Mohan and his wife die in the same accident, the policy money goes to his son.

Convertible Term Insurance

A type of term life policy that includes a provision allowing the policyholder to convert it to a permanent policy without providing evidence of insurability.

Example: At age 30, Maria buys a 15-year convertible term policy. At age 44, her health has declined, but she uses the conversion option to switch to a whole life policy without needing a new medical exam.

Corridor

In Universal Life policies, this is the minimum amount of pure insurance protection that must be maintained above the cash value for the policy to qualify as life insurance under tax law.

Example: If a universal life policy's cash value grows to ₹95,000, and the death benefit is ₹100,000, the corridor is only ₹5,000. The insurer might automatically increase the death benefit to maintain the required corridor and its tax status.

Cost Basis

The total amount of premiums paid into a cash value policy. When a policy is surrendered, any amount received above the cost basis is typically taxable.

Example: Over 20 years, John paid a total of ₹4 lakh in premiums for his policy. This ₹4 lakh is his cost basis. If he surrenders the policy for ₹5 lakh, he will have to pay tax on the ₹1 lakh gain.

Cost of Insurance

The charge deducted from a policy's cash value (especially in Universal Life) to cover the pure cost of the death benefit protection for that period. Also known as the mortality charge.

Example: Each month, ₹50 is deducted from Sarah's Universal Life policy's cash value. This ₹50 is the Cost of Insurance for that month, covering the insurer's risk of her passing away.

Coverage

The amount of risk or liability that is covered for an individual by insurance. Another term for Sum Assured or Death Benefit.

Example: A person says, "I have life insurance coverage of ₹1 crore," meaning their policy will pay out ₹1 crore upon their death.

Credit Life Insurance

A type of life insurance designed to pay off a borrower's specific debt (like a car loan or mortgage) if they die before it is fully paid.

Example: When buying a new car on loan, the bank offers Sameer a credit life insurance policy. If Sameer dies, the policy will pay the remaining car loan balance directly to the bank, so his family doesn't inherit the debt.

Critical Illness Rider

An add-on that pays a lump-sum benefit if the insured is diagnosed with a specific serious illness listed in the policy.

Example: Liam adds a critical illness rider to his life policy. When he is diagnosed with cancer, the rider pays him ₹10 lakh immediately, which he can use for treatment costs, independent of the main life insurance benefit.

Cross Purchase Plan

A type of buy-sell agreement where each business partner buys a life insurance policy on each of the other partners.

Example: In a three-partner firm (A, B, C), A buys policies on B and C, B buys policies on A and C, and C buys policies on A and B. This is a cross purchase plan to fund their buy-sell agreement.

Cumulative Premium

The total amount of premiums paid on a policy from its inception to the current date.

Example: If a policyholder has been paying an annual premium of ₹20,000 for 5 years, the cumulative premium paid is ₹1,00,000.

D

Daily Hospital Cash

A rider benefit that provides a fixed daily cash amount for each day the insured is hospitalized.

Example: Fatima has a daily hospital cash rider of ₹2,000 per day. When she is hospitalized for 5 days for a minor surgery, the insurer pays her ₹10,000 to cover miscellaneous expenses, regardless of the actual hospital bill.

Death Benefit

The amount of money paid to the beneficiary when the insured person dies.

Example: Arjun had a life insurance policy with a death benefit of ₹50 lakh. Upon his death, his wife, the beneficiary, received a cheque for ₹50 lakh from the insurance company.

Death Claim

The formal request submitted by a beneficiary to an insurance company to receive the policy's death benefit.

Example: After her father passed away, Priya gathered his policy documents, the death certificate, and her own ID proof to submit the death claim to the insurance office.

Declined Risk

An applicant for insurance whom the insurer has refused to cover due to excessively high risk.

Example: A person with a history of multiple heart attacks and uncontrolled diabetes applies for life insurance. The underwriter assesses them as a declined risk and refuses to issue a policy.

Declinature

The formal refusal by an insurance company to offer coverage to an applicant.

Example: After reviewing the medical reports, the insurance company sent the applicant a letter of declinature, officially informing him that his application for insurance was not accepted.

Decreasing Term Insurance

A type of term insurance where the death benefit decreases over the policy term, often at a predetermined rate. It's commonly used to cover a loan balance.

Example: Rajesh takes a 20-year home loan of ₹50 lakh. He also buys a decreasing term insurance policy for the same amount and term. As he repays his loan, the insurance coverage amount also reduces, matching the outstanding loan balance.

Deductible

The amount of a covered loss that the policyholder must pay out-of-pocket before the insurer begins to pay. More common in health and property insurance.

Example: A health insurance policy has a ₹5,000 deductible. If the policyholder has a hospital bill of ₹40,000, they must pay the first ₹5,000, and the insurance company will cover the remaining ₹35,000.

Deferred Annuity

An annuity contract in which the income payments begin at a future date, after an accumulation period.

Example: At age 45, Sunita buys a deferred annuity plan. She pays premiums until age 60. The pension payments are 'deferred' and will only start after she turns 60.

Deferred Compensation

A plan where an employer promises to pay an employee a portion of their salary at a later date, often upon retirement, sometimes funded by life insurance.

Example: A company's CEO is offered a deferred compensation package. The company buys a life insurance policy on her life. When she retires, the policy's cash value is used to pay her the promised income.

Deferred Premium

The portion of an annual premium that has not yet been paid because the policyholder has chosen a more frequent payment mode (e.g., monthly).

Example: A policy's annual premium is ₹12,000. The owner pays monthly (₹1,000). At the end of the first month, ₹1,000 is considered 'earned premium' and the remaining ₹11,000 is the 'deferred premium' for that year.

Delinquent Premium

A premium that has not been paid by its due date and is now in the grace period.

Example: John's premium was due on March 1st. It is now March 15th, and he still hasn't paid. His payment is considered a delinquent premium.

Dependent Coverage

Insurance coverage for the dependents of the primary insured, such as a spouse or children, often available as a rider on the main policy.

Example: On his own life insurance policy, Vikram adds a dependent coverage rider to provide a small amount of term life insurance for his wife and two children.

Direct Writer

An insurance company that sells its policies directly to the public through its own employees or exclusive agents, rather than through independent brokers.

Example: Many online-only insurance companies are direct writers. Customers buy policies from their website or call center employees without involving a third-party agent or broker.

Disability Benefit

A benefit, often in the form of a rider, that provides income or waives premiums if the insured becomes totally disabled.

Example: After a severe accident leaves him unable to work, Ahmed's policy's disability benefit kicks in. The insurance company starts paying him a monthly income and also waives all future premium payments for his life policy.

Disability Income Rider

An add-on to a life insurance policy that provides a monthly income to the insured if they become totally disabled and unable to work.

Example: A surgeon adds a disability income rider to his life policy. When an injury to his hand prevents him from ever performing surgery again, the rider starts paying him ₹50,000 per month.

Disclosure

The act of providing all required and relevant information, especially by the applicant during the application process or by the insurer about the policy terms.

Example: Full disclosure means that an applicant must honestly state their smoking habits, health conditions, and any risky hobbies on the insurance application.

Dividend

A share of the insurer's surplus (profits) paid to the owners of participating life insurance policies. Dividends are not guaranteed.

Example: Because the insurance company had a profitable year, it declared a dividend. Policyholders like Maria, who own participating policies, received a portion of this profit, which she chose to use to reduce her next premium payment.

Double Indemnity

A feature (often a rider) that provides for the payment of twice the policy's face amount if the insured dies as a result of an accident. It's a type of Accidental Death Benefit.

Example: A policy has a face amount of ₹20 lakh and a double indemnity rider. If the insured dies from a disease, the payout is ₹20 lakh. If they die in a train accident, the payout is doubled to ₹40 lakh.

Dread Disease Rider

An older term for a Critical Illness Rider, providing a benefit for specific, named diseases like cancer.

Example: In the 1980s, a person might have bought a 'dread disease rider' that specifically covered illnesses like polio or smallpox, which were more common concerns at the time.

E

Earned Premium

The portion of a premium that corresponds to the period of coverage that has already passed. The insurer has "earned" this part of the premium.

Example: If you pay a ₹1,200 annual premium on January 1st, by April 1st, three months have passed. The insurance company has 'earned' ₹300 of your premium. The remaining ₹900 is 'unearned'.

Effective Date

The date on which insurance coverage begins.

Example: Although Maya signed her application on July 10th, the policy was approved and issued with an effective date of August 1st. Her coverage officially started on August 1st.

Elimination Period

A waiting period after a disability begins before disability income benefits become payable. It functions like a deductible in time rather than money.

Example: A disability income policy has a 90-day elimination period. If the policyholder becomes disabled on January 1st, they will not receive their first income payment until after April 1st.

Endorsement

A written amendment or rider added to an insurance policy that changes its terms, coverage, or conditions.

Example: After getting married, Raj wants to change his wife's name on the policy. The insurance company issues an endorsement, which is a formal document attached to the original policy reflecting this change.

Endowment / Endowment Insurance

A life insurance policy that pays a lump sum (maturity benefit) if the insured survives the policy term, or a death benefit if the insured dies during the term.

Example: Priya buys a 20-year endowment policy. If she is alive after 20 years, she gets the maturity amount. If she passes away in the 15th year, her family gets the death benefit.

Entire Contract Clause

A standard provision stating that the life insurance policy, along with the attached application, constitutes the entire agreement between the policyholder and the insurer.

Example: If an agent verbally promised a benefit that is not written in the policy document, the entire contract clause means that promise is not legally binding. Only what's written in the policy and application matters.

Entry Age

The age of the insured at the time the policy is issued. This is the age used to calculate the premium.

Example: Two people buy the same policy. One person's entry age is 30, so their premium is ₹15,000. The other person's entry age is 40, so their premium is higher, at ₹22,000.

Evidence of Insurability

Proof of a person's physical and/or mental health, lifestyle, and other factors required by the insurer to determine if they are an acceptable risk for insurance.

Example: To reinstate a lapsed policy, the insurer asks the policyholder to provide fresh evidence of insurability, which means he has to undergo a new medical examination.

Ex Gratia Payment

A payment made by an insurer for a claim where there is no legal liability to pay. It is done voluntarily out of goodwill, without admitting liability.

Example: A claim is technically invalid due to a minor procedural error by the policyholder. To maintain a good relationship, the insurance company decides to make an ex gratia payment to the family anyway.

Excess Interest

Interest credited to a policy's cash value that is over and above the policy's guaranteed minimum interest rate.

Example: A universal life policy guarantees a 4% interest rate. Because the company's investments did well, they credit the policy with 6% interest for the year. The 2% above the guarantee is the excess interest.

Exclusion

A specific condition or circumstance listed in the policy for which the insurer will not provide coverage or pay a benefit.

Example: Most life insurance policies have a suicide exclusion for the first year. This means if the insured dies by suicide within 12 months of buying the policy, the death benefit will not be paid.

Expiry Date

The date on which an insurance policy's coverage ends.

Example: A 20-year term life policy bought on May 1, 2004, will have an expiry date of May 1, 2024. After this date, the policy provides no more coverage.

Extended Term Insurance

A non-forfeiture option where the cash surrender value of a policy is used to purchase a term insurance policy for the same face amount. The coverage lasts for as long a period as the cash value can buy.

Example: David stops paying premiums on his ₹10 lakh whole life policy. He uses the ₹50,000 cash value to choose the extended term insurance option. This buys him a ₹10 lakh term policy that remains active for another 8 years and 4 months, after which it expires.

Extra Premium

An additional premium charged to a policyholder who is classified as a higher-than-average risk (substandard risk).

Example: Because an applicant works in a hazardous job as a miner, the insurance company charges him the standard premium plus an extra premium of ₹1,500 per year to cover the additional risk.

F

Face Amount / Face Value

The basic death benefit of a life insurance policy as stated on the first page of the policy document, before any adjustments for riders or bonuses.

Example: The policy schedule states the "Face Amount" is ₹25,00,000. This is the guaranteed base amount of the death benefit.

Facility of Payment Clause

A clause that allows the insurer to pay a portion of the death benefit to a person who has incurred medical or funeral expenses for the insured, even if they are not the named beneficiary.

Example: An elderly man passes away. His neighbor paid for the initial funeral home expenses. The Facility of Payment Clause allows the insurer to directly reimburse the neighbor from the policy proceeds before paying the rest to the beneficiary who lives in another city.

Family Income Policy

A policy that combines whole life insurance with a decreasing term rider. If the insured dies during the term, it provides a monthly income to the family for the remainder of the term, plus the whole life benefit at the end.

Example: John buys a 20-year family income policy. If he dies in year 5, his family gets a monthly income for the remaining 15 years, and then a lump sum at the end of the 20-year period.

Family Maintenance Policy

A policy that combines whole life insurance with a level term rider. If the insured dies during the term, it provides a monthly income for a fixed period (e.g., 20 years) and pays the whole life benefit at the end of that income period.

Example: Jane buys a family maintenance policy. If she dies within the term, her family gets a monthly income for a full 20 years from the date of her death, plus a lump sum at the end of those 20 years.

Family Protection Policy

A policy that provides coverage for the entire family, usually including a main policy on the primary earner and smaller term policies on the spouse and children.

Example: The Kumar family buys a single Family Protection Policy. It provides ₹50 lakh cover for Mr. Kumar, ₹10 lakh for Mrs. Kumar, and ₹5 lakh for each of their two children, all under one contract.

Family Rider

An add-on to a life insurance policy that provides term insurance coverage for the insured's spouse and/or children.

Example: On her own ₹1 crore policy, Anika adds a family rider that gives her husband ₹20 lakh of term cover and her son ₹10 lakh of term cover for a small additional premium.

Fiduciary

A person or institution in a position of trust, legally and ethically obligated to act in the best interest of another person (the beneficiary). An insurer has a fiduciary duty to its policyholders.

Example: A trustee managing a life insurance trust for a minor child is a fiduciary. They must manage and distribute the policy proceeds solely for the child's benefit, not their own.

Final Expense Insurance

A type of whole life insurance with a small death benefit (e.g., $5,000 to $25,000) designed to cover final expenses such as medical bills and funeral costs.

Example: A 75-year-old man with no other savings buys a ₹2 lakh final expense insurance policy to ensure his funeral costs don't become a burden on his children.

First-To-Die Policy

A type of joint life insurance that covers two or more lives and pays the death benefit upon the death of the first person insured, after which the policy terminates.

Example: A married couple, Tom and Jerry, buy a first-to-die policy to cover their mortgage. When Tom passes away, the policy pays out to Jerry, who uses the money to clear the home loan. The policy then ends.

Fixed Amount Option

A settlement option where the death benefit is paid out in a series of fixed-amount installments until the principal and interest are exhausted.

Example: A ₹50 lakh death benefit is paid using the fixed amount option. The beneficiary chooses to receive ₹50,000 per month. The payments will continue until the entire ₹50 lakh, plus any interest earned, has been paid out.

Fixed Period Option

A settlement option where the death benefit and interest are paid out in installments over a chosen, fixed period of time.

Example: A beneficiary chooses the fixed period option for a ₹1 crore death benefit, selecting a period of 10 years. The insurance company calculates a fixed monthly payment that will be paid for exactly 10 years, after which the funds will be exhausted.

Fixed Premium

A premium that remains the same for the entire duration of the policy's premium-paying period.

Example: A whole life policy has a fixed premium of ₹25,000 per year. The policyholder will pay exactly ₹25,000 every year, from age 30 until they pass away, without any increase.

Flat Extra Premium

A constant additional charge per $1,000 of insurance added to a standard premium to cover a specific extra risk, such as a hazardous occupation.

Example: For a ₹10 lakh policy, an insurer adds a flat extra premium of ₹5 per thousand due to a health condition. This results in an additional annual charge of ₹5,000 (1000 x ₹5) on top of the base premium.

Forfeiture

The loss of a policy, its benefits, or its cash value due to the policyholder's failure to meet their obligations, such as paying premiums.

Example: After paying premiums for only one year, Mike stopped paying. Since the policy had not yet acquired a non-forfeiture value, he faced complete forfeiture, losing both the coverage and the premium he had paid.

Free Look Period

A mandatory period (usually 15-30 days) after a policy is delivered, during which the policyholder can review the policy and cancel it for a full refund of premiums paid.

Example: Riya receives her new policy document on March 1st. She has a 15-day free look period. After reading the terms, she decides she doesn't like it and returns the policy on March 10th for a full refund.

Front-End Load

A sales charge or commission deducted from the initial premium payments, typically in certain types of universal life policies or annuities.

Example: In a ULIP with a front-end load, if you pay a premium of ₹1,00,000, only ₹95,000 might be invested in the funds, with the remaining ₹5,000 being deducted as charges.

Fronting

An arrangement where a primary insurer issues a policy and then cedes most or all of the risk to a reinsurer, often to comply with local regulations.

Example: A multinational company wants to use its global insurer, 'Global Insure,' for its Indian employees. Since 'Global Insure' is not licensed in India, it arranges a fronting deal where a licensed Indian insurer issues the policy and then reinsures 100% of the risk back to 'Global Insure'.

G

Grace Period

A specified period (usually 30 or 31 days) after a premium's due date during which the policy remains in force and the payment can be made without the policy lapsing.

Example: A premium is due on April 1st. The policy has a 30-day grace period. If the insured dies on April 25th without having paid, the policy is still active, and the insurer will pay the death benefit after deducting the unpaid premium.

Graded Premium Life Insurance

A type of whole life policy where premiums start low and increase annually for a set number of years before leveling off for the remainder of the policy.

Example: A young doctor buys a graded premium policy. The premium is only ₹10,000 in the first year, increasing by ₹2,000 each year for 10 years. From the 11th year onwards, it becomes a fixed level premium of ₹30,000 for life.

Group Life Insurance

A single life insurance policy that covers a group of people, such as employees of a company. Coverage is often offered at a lower cost and may not require a medical exam.

Example: A large IT company provides all its 5,000 employees with group life insurance coverage of three times their annual salary as part of their employee benefits package.

Group Term Insurance

Term life insurance coverage provided to members of a group, typically for one year and renewable annually.

Example: A bank provides its credit card holders with an optional group term insurance plan. Members can opt-in to get ₹5 lakh of term cover for a low annual premium.

Group Underwriting

The process of assessing the risk of an entire group, rather than individuals, to determine the terms and premium for a group policy.

Example: When insuring a company's employees, the underwriter looks at the average age, gender ratio, and occupation of the group as a whole, rather than requiring individual medical exams for everyone.

Guaranteed Insurability

A rider that allows the policyholder to purchase additional amounts of insurance at specified future dates without having to provide evidence of insurability.

Example: A young professional buys a policy with a guaranteed insurability rider. This allows him to buy an extra ₹10 lakh of coverage when he gets married and another ₹10 lakh when his first child is born, without any new medical questions.

Guaranteed Issue

A policy that is issued to any eligible applicant without a medical exam or health questions. Premiums are typically higher and death benefits may be graded for the first few years.

Example: An airline offers all its frequent flyers a guaranteed issue life insurance policy of ₹5 lakh. Anyone who applies is accepted, regardless of their health.

Guaranteed Renewable

A policy provision that guarantees the policyholder the right to renew their coverage at the end of the term, without evidence of insurability. Premiums for the new term may be higher.

Example: A 10-year term policy is guaranteed renewable. At the end of 10 years, the policyholder can continue the coverage for another 10 years by paying the higher premium applicable to their new age, without a medical check-up.

Guaranteed Surrender Value

The minimum cash surrender value that a policy is guaranteed to have at specified points in time, as shown in the policy's tables.

Example: The illustration table in an endowment policy shows that at the end of the 10th year, the Guaranteed Surrender Value will be ₹2,50,000. This is the minimum amount the policyholder would get if they cancel the policy then.

Guarantor

A person or entity that provides a guarantee; in insurance, this can refer to state guaranty associations that protect policyholders if an insurer becomes insolvent.

Example: If a life insurance company goes bankrupt, the state's insurance guarantor association steps in to ensure that policyholders still receive their claim payments, up to a certain legal limit.

H

Hazard

A condition or situation that increases the probability or severity of a loss. Examples include smoking (physical hazard) or dishonesty (moral hazard).

Example: For a life insurer, smoking is a physical hazard because it increases the chance of death. A history of filing fraudulent claims is a moral hazard because it indicates a risk of dishonesty.

Hazardous Occupation Clause

A policy provision that may limit or exclude benefits if the insured's death is a result of their high-risk job.

Example: A policy issued to a bomb disposal expert might contain a hazardous occupation clause, stating that the accidental death benefit will not be paid if death occurs while on duty.

Health Declaration / Health Statement

A statement signed by the applicant declaring their current health status and medical history, used as part of the underwriting process.

Example: For a non-medical policy, the applicant must sign a health declaration form, confirming that they do not suffer from any major illnesses and are in good health.

Health Questionnaire

A form with questions about an applicant's health, medical history, and lifestyle, used to assess their risk level.

Example: The health questionnaire asks, "Have you been treated for heart disease in the last 5 years?" and "Do you consume tobacco products?". The answers help the insurer classify the risk.

Human Life Value

An approach to determining the amount of life insurance needed, based on the present value of an individual's future net earnings that would be lost if they were to die.

Example: An advisor calculates a client's Human Life Value. The client earns ₹10 lakh a year and has 20 years to retirement. The advisor calculates the present value of this future income stream to suggest an appropriate insurance amount.

Hypothecation

The act of pledging an asset (like a life insurance policy's cash value) as collateral for a loan, without transferring ownership of the asset.

Example: When you take a loan against your life insurance policy, you are technically engaging in hypothecation. You still own the policy, but you have pledged its value to the insurer as security for the loan.

I

Immediate Annuity

An annuity that begins making income payments to the annuitant shortly after it is purchased, typically within one year.

Example: Upon retiring at age 60, Mr. Gupta invests his ₹1 crore retirement fund into an immediate annuity plan. Starting the very next month, he begins receiving a pension of ₹60,000 for life.

Immediate Cover

Temporary insurance coverage that begins immediately upon the signing of the application and payment of the first premium, as provided by a binding receipt.

Example: An agent tells a client, "Once you pay the first premium and I give you this receipt, you get immediate cover, so you are protected from this moment onwards."

Impaired Risk

An applicant whose physical condition, medical history, or other factors pose a higher-than-average risk of loss to the insurer. They may be charged a higher premium or declined coverage.

Example: A person with a history of heart disease is considered an impaired risk by the insurance company and is offered a policy at a 50% higher premium than a healthy individual.

Implied Authority

Authority that is not explicitly granted to an agent in their contract but that is assumed necessary to conduct their business (e.g., using company stationery).

Example: An agent's contract doesn't explicitly say they can print business cards with the company logo, but this authority is implied for them to perform their job effectively.

Incontestability Clause

A mandatory clause that prevents the insurer from contesting the validity of a policy (except for non-payment of premiums) after it has been in force for a specific period, usually two years.

Example: John lied on his application but dies in the third year of the policy. Because the two-year incontestability period is over, the insurer cannot dispute the claim based on the initial lie and must pay the death benefit.

Incurred But Not Reported (IBNR)

An estimated reserve that an insurer sets aside for claims that have likely occurred but have not yet been reported to the company.

Example: An insurer knows that, statistically, some people who died in December won't have their claims filed until January. They create an IBNR reserve in their December financial statements to account for these expected but not-yet-filed claims.

Indemnity

A principle of insurance that states the insurer will restore the insured or beneficiary to the financial position they were in before the loss occurred, but not allow them to profit from the loss.

Example: If a fire destroys a car worth ₹5 lakh, the principle of indemnity means the car insurance will pay up to ₹5 lakh to cover the loss, not ₹10 lakh, as that would allow the owner to profit from the event.

Indexed Universal Life (IUL)

A type of universal life insurance where the cash value growth is tied to the performance of a stock market index (like the S&P 500), with both a floor (guaranteed minimum return) and a cap (maximum potential return).

Example: In an IUL policy, if the NIFTY 50 index goes up 15% in a year and the policy has a 10% cap, the cash value is credited with 10% growth. If the index falls 20%, the cash value is protected by a 0% floor and doesn't lose value.

Individual Insurance

A policy that provides coverage for a single individual (and sometimes their family), as opposed to group insurance.

Example: Sarah's employer provides group insurance, but she wants more coverage. She buys an additional individual insurance policy directly from an insurance company that is tailored to her specific needs.

In-force Policy

An active insurance policy for which premiums are being paid and coverage is in effect.

Example: A policyholder is up-to-date with all their premium payments. Their policy is currently in-force, meaning if they were to pass away, the claim would be payable.

Insurable Interest

A fundamental requirement for issuing a policy, where the policy owner must be likely to suffer a genuine financial or emotional loss from the death of the insured.

Example: A person can buy life insurance on their spouse because they have a clear insurable interest. However, they cannot buy life insurance on a random stranger because they would not suffer a financial loss from that stranger's death.

Insurance

A contract (policy) in which an individual or entity receives financial protection or reimbursement against losses from an insurance company.

Example: By paying a small annual premium for car insurance, a car owner transfers the large financial risk of a potential accident to the insurance company.

Insurance Ombudsman

An impartial, government-appointed official who investigates and resolves complaints from policyholders against insurance companies.

Example: If an insurance company wrongly rejects a claim and refuses to reconsider, the policyholder can file a complaint with the Insurance Ombudsman for a fair and free resolution.

Insurance Rider

An optional add-on to an insurance policy that provides additional benefits or modifies the terms of the policy, often for an extra premium. (Same as Rider).

Example: Arjun adds a Critical Illness insurance rider to his term plan. This gives him extra protection for specific illnesses on top of his basic life cover.

Insured

The person whose life is covered by the insurance policy.

Example: A father buys a life insurance policy for his son to cover his education costs. The father is the policy owner, but the son is the insured.

Insurer

The insurance company that provides the coverage and is obligated to pay claims.

Example: Life Insurance Corporation of India (LIC) is the insurer that issues the policy and promises to pay the death benefit.

Interest-Sensitive Insurance

A type of permanent life insurance where the cash value growth is credited with an interest rate that can change over time, reflecting the insurer's current investment earnings.

Example: A universal life policy is interest-sensitive. It might guarantee a minimum of 3% interest but could pay as high as 7% in years when the insurer's investments perform exceptionally well.

Irrevocable Beneficiary

A beneficiary designation that cannot be changed by the policy owner without the written consent of the beneficiary themselves.

Example: A court orders a husband to name his ex-wife as an irrevocable beneficiary on his life insurance policy as part of a divorce settlement. He cannot remove her name from the policy later without her permission.

Issue Age

The age of the insured on the policy's effective date, which is used as the basis for calculating the premium.

Example: Even though David applied for the policy when he was 29, it was only issued after his 30th birthday. His issue age is 30, and his premium is calculated based on that age.

Issuer

The insurance company that issues and underwrites the policy.

Example: In the context of a life insurance contract, the issuer is the company whose name and logo are on the policy document, such as HDFC Life or SBI Life.

J

Joint and Survivor Annuity

An annuity that makes payments to two or more annuitants (usually a married couple) and continues to make payments to the surviving annuitant after the first one dies.

Example: Mr. and Mrs. Sharma buy a joint and survivor annuity. They receive a pension as long as both are alive. When Mr. Sharma passes away, Mrs. Sharma continues to receive the pension for the rest of her life.

Joint Life Insurance

A single policy that covers two or more lives. It can be a First-to-Die policy or a Last-to-Die (Survivorship) policy.

Example: Two business partners take a joint life insurance policy to fund their buy-sell agreement. The policy pays out on the death of the first partner, providing funds for the survivor to buy the deceased partner's shares.

Joint Term Insurance

A term insurance policy that covers two lives, typically paying the death benefit upon the first death.

Example: A young couple with a joint home loan buys a joint term insurance policy. If either of them dies during the loan period, the policy pays out, allowing the survivor to pay off the mortgage.

Juvenile Insurance

Life insurance purchased on the life of a minor child, typically by a parent or grandparent.

Example: A grandfather buys a juvenile insurance policy for his newborn grandson to create a savings fund for his future education and to guarantee his insurability later in life.

K

Key Person (or Key Man) Insurance

Life or disability insurance purchased by a business on a key employee, whose death or disability would cause a significant financial loss to the company. The business is the beneficiary.

Example: A software company's success depends on its lead programmer. The company buys a ₹2 crore Key Person insurance policy on her life. If she dies, the company receives the money to hire a replacement and manage the business disruption.

Knock-for-Knock Agreement

An agreement between two insurance companies to not pursue subrogation against each other. More common in auto insurance.

Example: In a car accident involving drivers from two different insurance companies who have a knock-for-knock agreement, each company pays for its own client's damages, regardless of who was at fault, to avoid costly legal battles.

Knock-Out Option

A feature in some indexed products where if the index performs exceptionally well, the policyholder receives a predetermined high return, but may forgo further gains for that period.

Example: An indexed annuity has a knock-out option at 15%. If the market index gains 25% in a year, the policyholder's account is credited with the 15% return, and any further gain for that year is 'knocked out'.

L

Lapsed Policy

A policy that has been terminated and is no longer in force due to non-payment of premiums after the grace period has expired.

Example: Ravi missed his premium payment due on January 1st. His 30-day grace period ended on January 31st. Since he didn't pay by then, his policy became a lapsed policy on February 1st, and he no longer has life insurance coverage.

Last-to-Die Policy (Survivorship)

A joint life insurance policy that covers two or more lives and pays the death benefit only after the last insured person dies. Often used for estate planning.

Example: A wealthy couple buys a last-to-die policy. When the first spouse dies, nothing is paid. When the second spouse dies years later, the policy pays out a large sum to their children, which they use to pay inheritance taxes.

Level Premium

A premium that remains fixed and does not change throughout the premium-paying period of the policy.

Example: A term insurance policy has a level premium of ₹1,000 per month. The policyholder pays exactly ₹1,000 every month for the entire 20-year term of the policy.

Level Term

A type of term insurance where the death benefit remains the same throughout the entire policy term.

Example: In a level term policy with a ₹50 lakh Sum Assured, the death benefit will be ₹50 lakh whether the insured passes away in the 1st year or the 19th year of a 20-year policy.

Lien

A legal claim against an asset (like a policy's death benefit) as security for a debt. For example, a graded death benefit policy might have a lien against it for the first few years.

Example: An insurer issues a policy to a high-risk individual but places a 50% lien on it for the first 3 years. If the person dies within that period, the insurer has a legal right to hold back 50% of the death benefit.

Lien Clause

A policy provision, often in graded benefit policies, that reduces the death benefit if death occurs within the first few years of the policy.

Example: A guaranteed issue policy has a lien clause stating that if death by natural causes occurs in the first two years, the benefit paid will only be a refund of premiums plus interest, not the full face amount.

Life Annuity

An annuity that provides periodic income payments for the entire lifetime of the annuitant. Payments cease upon the annuitant's death (unless there is a survivor or period certain feature).

Example: With a 'life annuity' option, if the pensioner receives payments for 20 years and then passes away, the payments stop completely. There is no refund or further payment to the family.

Life Expectancy

The average number of years a person of a certain age is expected to live, based on mortality tables.

Example: According to the insurer's tables, the life expectancy of a healthy 60-year-old male is 82 years, meaning he is expected to live for another 22 years on average.

Life Insurance

A contract between a policy owner and an insurer, where the insurer agrees to pay a designated beneficiary a sum of money upon the death of the insured person.

Example: A mother buys a life insurance policy to ensure that if she passes away unexpectedly, there will be money available to fund her children's college education.

Life Settlement

The sale of an existing life insurance policy by the policy owner to a third-party company for more than its cash surrender value but less than its face amount.

Example: An elderly man no longer needs his ₹50 lakh policy. The surrender value is ₹5 lakh. A life settlement company offers him ₹10 lakh in cash for the policy. The company now owns the policy, pays the premiums, and will collect the full ₹50 lakh when the man passes away.

Limited Benefit Policy

An insurance policy that only covers specific, named perils or conditions, as opposed to a comprehensive policy.

Example: A 'cancer care' policy is a limited benefit policy because it will only pay a claim if the insured is diagnosed with cancer, and not for any other illness like a heart attack or stroke.

Limited Payment Life

A type of whole life insurance where premiums are paid for a limited number of years (e.g., 20 years or until age 65), but the coverage remains in force for the insured's entire life.

Example: A person buys a "20-Pay" limited payment life policy. They pay premiums for only 20 years (from age 30 to 50). After age 50, they pay nothing more, but the life insurance coverage continues for the rest of their life.

Living Benefits

Benefits from a life insurance policy that the insured can access while still alive, such as loans, surrender value, or accelerated death benefits for terminal illness.

Example: After being diagnosed with a terminal illness, John uses the living benefits feature of his policy to access 50% of his death benefit immediately to pay for medical care and travel.

Loan Against Policy

A loan taken by the policy owner from the insurer, using the policy's cash value as collateral. It does not have to be repaid, but any outstanding balance plus interest will be deducted from the death benefit.

Example: Needing funds for a home repair, Sumit takes a loan against his policy of ₹1 lakh. When he passes away a few years later with the loan still unpaid, his nominee receives the death benefit minus the ₹1 lakh loan and any accrued interest.

Loading

The amount added to the net premium to cover the insurer's operating expenses, commissions, taxes, and a margin for profit and contingencies.

Example: The pure cost (net premium) to cover claims for a policy is ₹800. The insurer adds a loading of ₹200 to cover agent commissions, office rent, and profit, making the final gross premium ₹1,000.

Long Duration Contract

An insurance contract, such as life insurance, that is expected to remain in force for an extended period.

Example: A whole life insurance policy is a long duration contract because it is designed to last for many decades, unlike a one-year car insurance policy.

Long-Term Care Rider

An add-on that allows the policyholder to accelerate a portion of their death benefit to pay for long-term care services if they become chronically ill.

Example: An elderly woman can no longer perform daily activities like eating and dressing by herself. She activates the long-term care rider on her life policy, which pays her a monthly benefit to cover the cost of a full-time nurse.

M

Material Misrepresentation

A false statement made by an applicant on their insurance application that is significant enough that the insurer would have made a different decision (e.g., charged a higher premium or denied coverage) had they known the truth.

Example: An applicant states they are a non-smoker when they actually smoke two packs a day. This is a material misrepresentation because the insurer would have charged a much higher premium if they knew the truth.

Maturity Date

The date on which the face amount or cash value of a life insurance policy becomes payable to the policyholder, typically at the end of the policy term for an endowment policy.

Example: An endowment policy taken on June 1, 2004, for a 20-year term will have a maturity date of June 1, 2024, on which the survival benefits are paid out.

Medical Examiner

A physician or registered nurse who conducts the medical examination of an insurance applicant on behalf of the insurance company.

Example: As part of his life insurance application, a medical examiner visited Sameer's home to check his blood pressure, collect blood and urine samples, and ask about his medical history.

Medical Information Bureau (MIB)

A non-profit cooperative data exchange for North American insurance companies. It stores coded information from previous insurance applications to help prevent fraud and misrepresentation.

Example: An applicant applies to Insurer A and declares a health condition. He then applies to Insurer B and hides it. Insurer B checks the MIB database and sees a code indicating the condition from the first application, which helps them uncover the fraud.

Medical Underwriting

The process of an insurer evaluating an applicant's medical history, health status, and other health-related information to determine their risk profile and insurability.

Example: The medical underwriting team reviewed the applicant's blood test results, doctor's reports, and family health history before approving his policy at a standard rate.

Misstatement of Age or Sex Clause

A policy provision that states if the insured's age or sex was misstated on the application, the policy benefits will be adjusted to what the premium paid would have purchased at the correct age or sex.

Example: A man stated his age as 40 when it was actually 45. At the time of a claim, the insurer discovers this. Instead of denying the claim, they use the misstatement of age clause to calculate and pay a lower death benefit—the amount the premium would have bought for a 45-year-old.

Mode of Premium / Premium Mode

The frequency with which premiums are paid, such as annually, semi-annually, quarterly, or monthly.

Example: Anita chooses the 'monthly' mode of premium for her policy, so her premium is automatically debited from her bank account each month. Her friend chose the 'yearly' mode and pays a single, larger premium once a year.

Modified Endowment Contract (MEC)

A life insurance policy that has been funded with more money than allowed under federal tax laws (failing the 7-pay test). MECs lose some of the tax advantages of regular life insurance; withdrawals and loans are potentially taxable and subject to penalties.

Example: A policy requires a premium of ₹1 lakh per year. The policyholder decides to pay ₹10 lakh in the first year. This overfunding causes the policy to be classified as a Modified Endowment Contract, and any future withdrawals will be taxed differently.

Morale Hazard

A risk attitude of indifference or carelessness towards loss because of the existence of insurance. For example, engaging in risky behavior because "insurance will cover it."

Example: A person with excellent health insurance might be less inclined to maintain a healthy diet and exercise regimen, thinking "if I get sick, my insurance will pay for it." This attitude is a morale hazard.

Morbidity Rate

The frequency or incidence of a disease or illness within a population. It's a key factor in pricing health and disability insurance.

Example: To set the premium for a critical illness policy, an actuary studies the morbidity rate for cancer to predict how many policyholders out of 1,000 are likely to be diagnosed with the disease each year.

Mortality Charge

The cost of the pure life insurance protection in a policy. In universal life, it is a monthly deduction from the cash value to cover the death benefit.

Example: Out of a ₹5,000 monthly premium for a ULIP, ₹4,500 is invested, and ₹500 is deducted as the mortality charge to provide the life cover for that month.

Mortality Risk

The risk that an individual will die sooner than expected, which is the primary risk an insurer assumes in a life insurance policy.

Example: An insurance company faces mortality risk because if a large number of its policyholders die earlier than projected, the company will have to pay out more in claims than it collected in premiums.

Mortality Table

A statistical chart that shows the death rate at each age, based on a large population, used by actuaries to calculate premiums and reserves.

Example: An actuary uses a mortality table to determine the net premium for a 30-year-old applicant, noting the probability of death for each year of the policy term.

Mutual Insurance Company

An insurance company that is owned by its policyholders. Profits are returned to policyholders in the form of dividends or reduced premiums.

Example: Since LIC is a mutual corporation (historically), its policyholders who own 'participating' plans are entitled to a share of the company's profits in the form of bonuses and dividends.

N

Named Beneficiary

A beneficiary who is specifically identified by name in the policy document.

Example: The policy states, "The beneficiary is my son, Rahul Sharma." This makes Rahul the named beneficiary, as opposed to a vague designation like "my children."

Net Amount at Risk

The difference between the policy's death benefit and its accumulated cash value. This represents the actual amount of pure insurance the company is liable for at any given time.

Example: A policy has a death benefit of ₹10 lakh and a cash value of ₹3 lakh. The insurer's Net Amount at Risk is ₹7 lakh. If the insured dies, ₹3 lakh comes from the policy's own savings, and the insurer pays the remaining ₹7 lakh.

Net Premium

The portion of the premium that is calculated to cover only the cost of expected claims (mortality costs), based on a mortality table and an assumed interest rate. It does not include the loading for expenses.

Example: The actuary calculates that the pure cost to cover future claims for a policy is ₹8,000 per year. This is the net premium. The company then adds ₹2,000 for expenses to arrive at the final ₹10,000 gross premium.

Nominee

The person designated by the policyholder to receive the benefits of the policy in the event of the insured's death. Essentially the same as a beneficiary.

Example: In his bank account and his life insurance policy, Sameer appoints his wife as the nominee, ensuring she will receive the funds upon his demise.

Non-Contributory Plan

A group insurance plan where the employer pays 100% of the premium, and employees do not contribute.

Example: At a large tech company, every employee is automatically enrolled in the group life insurance plan, and the premium is fully paid by the company. This is a non-contributory plan.

Non-Forfeiture Options

The choices available to a policyholder if they stop paying premiums on a cash value policy. Options typically include surrendering the policy for its cash value, converting to reduced paid-up insurance, or converting to extended term insurance.

Example: After 15 years, Ajay can no longer afford his premiums. He contacts the insurer and chooses one of the non-forfeiture options: taking the cash surrender value and ending the policy.

Non-Medical Policy

A life insurance policy issued without requiring the applicant to undergo a medical examination, typically based on answers to a health questionnaire.

Example: A young, healthy student buys a ₹10 lakh non-medical policy online by simply filling out a form about her health and lifestyle, without needing to go for a blood test or medical check-up.

Non-Participating Policy

A policy that does not pay dividends to the policyholder. The premiums are generally lower than for participating policies.

Example: A pure term insurance plan is a non-participating policy. The policyholder is not entitled to any share of the company's profits; they only get the guaranteed death benefit.

Non-Resident Agent

An insurance agent who is licensed to sell insurance in a state where they do not legally reside.

Example: An agent who lives in Noida (UP) but also gets a license to sell insurance in Delhi is a non-resident agent when conducting business in Delhi.

Non-Smoker Rate

A lower premium rate offered to insurance applicants who do not use tobacco products, reflecting their lower mortality risk.

Example: Two applicants of the same age and health apply for the same policy. The one who smokes is quoted a premium of ₹20,000, while the other, who qualifies for the non-smoker rate, is quoted a premium of only ₹12,000.

Notice of Assignment

A formal document sent to the insurance company to inform them that the policy has been assigned to a third party.

Example: After assigning his policy to a bank for a loan, the policyholder must send a Notice of Assignment to the insurance company so they can officially record the bank's interest in the policy.

Notice of Claim

The formal notification given by the beneficiary to the insurance company that a loss (death of the insured) has occurred and that a claim will be submitted.

Example: The first step in the claims process is for the nominee to call the insurance company's helpline or visit a branch to give a Notice of Claim, informing them of the policyholder's passing.

NRI Policy

An insurance policy specifically designed for Non-Resident Indians (NRIs), with terms and conditions that cater to their status.

Example: An Indian citizen working in Dubai buys an NRI policy, which allows him to pay premiums in foreign currency and ensures a straightforward claim process for his family back in India.

O

Occupational Risk

The level of hazard associated with an applicant's job, which can affect their risk classification and premium.

Example: A person working as a window cleaner on skyscrapers has a much higher occupational risk than an office accountant, and their life insurance premium will be significantly higher.

Offer and Acceptance

The two key components of a valid contract. The applicant "offers" to buy insurance by submitting an application and premium. The insurer "accepts" the offer by issuing the policy.

Example: When you submit a signed application form with a cheque for the first premium, you are making an offer. When the insurance company issues the policy document, it is an acceptance of your offer, and the contract is formed.

Offshore Policy

An insurance policy issued by a company in a different jurisdiction or country than where the policyholder resides, often for tax or investment purposes.

Example: A high-net-worth individual might purchase an offshore policy based in a low-tax jurisdiction like the Isle of Man to take advantage of favorable investment and tax regulations.

Operational Risk

The risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, systems, or from external events.

Example: An insurance company faces operational risk if its computer systems crash, leading to an inability to process claims, or if an employee commits internal fraud.

Option to Renew

A right given to the policyholder to renew a policy at the end of its term without evidence of insurability.

Example: A 5-year health insurance policy has an option to renew. At the end of 5 years, even if the policyholder's health has worsened, they can continue the policy by simply paying the renewal premium.

Out-of-Force Policy

Another term for a lapsed policy; one that is no longer providing coverage.

Example: The agent informed the client that since premiums had not been paid for over 90 days, his policy was now out-of-force and would not provide any benefit if a claim arose.

Outstanding Loan

The total unpaid balance of a loan taken against a policy's cash value, including accrued interest.

Example: Five years ago, a policyholder took a ₹1 lakh loan. With interest, the outstanding loan balance is now ₹1.2 lakh. This amount will be deducted from any claim payout.

Overdue Premium

A premium that has not been paid by its due date and is currently within the grace period.

Example: The premium was due on May 1st. On May 10th, the company sends a reminder stating that the premium is now overdue and must be paid before the grace period ends on May 31st.

P

Paid-Up Additions

Additional small amounts of whole life insurance purchased using policy dividends. These additions have their own cash value and increase the policy's total death benefit.

Example: Instead of taking her annual dividend of ₹5,000 in cash, Sarah uses it to buy paid-up additions. This adds ₹20,000 of permanent life cover to her existing policy, increasing her total death benefit.

Paid-Up Insurance

A policy on which all required premiums have been paid, but which remains in force for the rest of the insured's life. This can be a limited-pay policy or a policy converted using a non-forfeiture option.

Example: After paying all premiums for his "20-Pay" life policy, the policy becomes a paid-up insurance. He no longer pays anything, but his life cover continues until his death.

Parametric Insurance

Insurance that pays out based on a pre-defined trigger or event (e.g., a hurricane of a certain category), rather than the magnitude of the actual loss.

Example: A farmer buys parametric insurance that pays ₹5 lakh if rainfall is less than 50cm during the monsoon season, regardless of his actual crop loss. The low rainfall is the trigger.

Participating Fund

The pool of assets from which an insurer pays bonuses or dividends on its participating policies.

Example: The premiums from all 'with-profit' endowment plans are invested in the company's participating fund. The returns generated by this fund are then used to declare annual bonuses for those policyholders.

Participating Policy

A type of life insurance policy that allows the policyholder to "participate" in the insurer's profits through the payment of dividends.

Example: An LIC endowment plan is a participating policy. This means that in addition to the guaranteed sum assured, the policyholder will also receive bonuses based on the company's performance.

Payment Mode

The frequency of premium payments, such as annually, semi-annually, quarterly, or monthly.

Example: When buying a policy, the applicant chose the 'Half-Yearly' payment mode, meaning they will pay their premium once every six months.

Payment of Claims Clause

A mandatory policy provision that requires the insurer to pay the death benefit promptly (usually within 30-60 days) after receiving satisfactory proof of death.

Example: The Payment of Claims Clause in the policy contract gives the beneficiary the legal right to expect the claim amount from the insurer within 30 days of submitting all the required documents.

Pension Plan

A retirement plan that provides a monthly income to retirees, often funded by employers and/or employees during their working years.

Example: Through a pension plan, an individual contributes regularly during their career. After retirement, the accumulated corpus is used to provide them with a steady monthly pension for life.

Per Capita Beneficiary

A beneficiary designation that means "by the head." The death benefit is divided equally among the surviving beneficiaries in a class. If one beneficiary predeceases the insured, their share is re-divided among the remaining beneficiaries.

Example: A man names his three children as per capita beneficiaries. If one child dies before him, the policy proceeds will be split equally (50/50) between the two surviving children.

Per Stirpes Beneficiary

A beneficiary designation that means "by the branch." If a beneficiary predeceases the insured, their share of the death benefit passes down to their own heirs (e.g., their children) rather than being divided among the other primary beneficiaries.

Example: A man names his three children as per stirpes beneficiaries. If one child dies before him, that child's share will pass to their own children (the man's grandchildren). The other two surviving children will still receive their original one-third share.

Permanent Life Insurance

A category of life insurance (including whole life and universal life) that provides lifelong coverage and includes a cash value savings component.

Example: Unlike a term plan that expires, a whole life policy is a type of permanent life insurance that provides coverage for the insured's entire life, as long as premiums are paid.

Persistency Ratio

A measure of how many policies remain in force over a certain period. A high persistency ratio is desirable as it indicates customer satisfaction and stable revenue for the insurer.

Example: An insurer has a 13th-month persistency ratio of 90%, which means that 90% of the policies sold last year are still active and have paid their first-year renewal premium.

Physical Hazard

A physical condition or characteristic of an individual that increases the chance of loss, such as a chronic health condition or a dangerous hobby.

Example: High blood pressure is a physical hazard for a life insurance applicant because it statistically increases their risk of a heart attack or stroke.

Policy

The legal written contract of insurance between the insurer and the policy owner.

Example: After his application was approved, John received a 20-page booklet in the mail. This document is his official insurance policy, outlining all the terms and benefits.

Policy Anniversary

The anniversary of the date the policy was issued.

Example: A policy issued on September 15, 2020, will have its policy anniversary on September 15th of every subsequent year. This date is important for premium payments and crediting of bonuses.

Policy Dividend

A return of a portion of the premium paid on a participating policy, representing a share of the insurer's surplus. (Same as Dividend).

Example: At the end of the year, the policyholder receives a notice that a policy dividend of ₹5,000 has been declared, which they can take as cash or use to pay part of their next premium.

Policy Document

The physical or digital contract that contains all the terms, conditions, coverages, and exclusions of the insurance agreement.

Example: When a claim is filed, the insurance company refers to the original policy document to verify the coverage, check for exclusions, and confirm the beneficiary details.

Policy Holder

The person or entity that owns the insurance policy and has the legal rights to make changes, such as changing beneficiaries or taking loans.

Example: A mother buys a policy on her daughter's life. The daughter is the 'insured', but the mother is the 'policy holder' because she pays the premiums and controls the policy.

Policy Loan

A loan made by the insurer to the policy owner, using the policy's cash value as collateral.

Example: To fund an emergency, a policyholder applies for a policy loan and receives a cheque from the insurance company, which he will repay over time or let it be deducted from the final claim amount.

Policy Reserves

A liability on the insurer's balance sheet representing the amount of money needed today, invested at an assumed interest rate, to pay all future claims on its policies.

Example: Insurance regulators require companies to maintain adequate policy reserves to ensure that even if the company stopped selling new policies today, it would still have enough money to pay the claims of all its existing customers in the future.

Policy Schedule

A section of the policy document, usually at the front, that summarizes all the key details: policy number, insured's name, face amount, premium, beneficiary, etc.

Example: Instead of reading the whole policy document, a person can look at the policy schedule to quickly find their sum assured, premium amount, and policy term.

Policy Term

The period of time for which the insurance policy provides coverage.

Example: A person buys a term insurance policy with a policy term of 30 years. The insurance coverage is only active during these 30 years and expires afterward.

Policy Year

The 12-month period beginning on the policy's effective date and on each anniversary of that date.

Example: A policy starts on March 10, 2023. The first policy year runs from March 10, 2023, to March 9, 2024. The second policy year begins on March 10, 2024.

Portability

A feature of some group life insurance plans that allows a departing employee to convert their group coverage into an individual policy without evidence of insurability.

Example: When Maria leaves her job, she uses the portability feature of her company's group plan to convert her ₹20 lakh group cover into an individual policy that she can continue by paying the premiums herself.

Pre-Authorisation

Approval required from an insurer before receiving certain medical services to ensure they are covered. More common in health insurance.

Example: Before a planned knee surgery, the hospital's insurance desk sends all the patient's documents to the insurer to get pre-authorisation for a cashless claim.

Pre-existing Condition

A health condition or illness that an individual had before the effective date of their insurance policy. Coverage for such conditions may be limited or excluded for a certain period.

Example: A person has had diabetes for 5 years before buying a health policy. Diabetes is a pre-existing condition, and the policy might not cover diabetes-related hospitalizations for the first 3 years.

Premium

The periodic payment made by the policyholder to the insurance company to keep the policy in force.

Example: To keep his ₹1 crore term insurance active, Rohan pays a premium of ₹15,000 to the insurance company every year.

Premium Holiday

A feature in some universal life policies that allows the policyholder to skip premium payments, as long as the policy's cash value is sufficient to cover the monthly deductions.

Example: After facing a temporary job loss, a policyholder uses the premium holiday feature to stop paying premiums for six months. The policy's monthly charges are instead deducted from its accumulated cash value, keeping the cover active.

Premium Paying Term

The specific duration over which the policyholder is required to pay premiums.

Example: In a "20-15" plan, the policy term is 20 years, but the premium paying term is only 15 years. The policyholder pays premiums for 15 years and then enjoys coverage for the full 20 years.

Primary Beneficiary

The person or entity first in line to receive the policy's death benefit.

Example: A man designates his wife as the primary beneficiary. If he dies, she is the one who will receive the insurance money. His children, named as contingent beneficiaries, will only get it if his wife has also passed away.

Primary Insured

The main person whose life is covered under the insurance policy.

Example: On a family floater health plan, the father might be the primary insured, with his wife and children covered as dependents under the same policy.

Proof of Death

Official documentation required by the insurer to process a claim, typically a certified copy of the death certificate.

Example: To process a life insurance claim, the nominee must submit the original policy, the claim form, and, most importantly, the proof of death issued by the municipal authorities.

Proof of Insurability

Evidence that a person meets the insurer's standards for health and lifestyle to be eligible for insurance coverage.

Example: When trying to increase his policy's coverage amount, the applicant had to provide fresh proof of insurability by undergoing a full medical check-up.

Proposer

The person who applies for the insurance policy. The proposer is usually the policy owner and may or may not be the person insured.

Example: A father fills out the application form for a life insurance policy on his daughter. The father is the proposer and policy owner, while the daughter is the life insured.

Protection Plan

A general term for an insurance policy designed primarily to provide a death benefit, as opposed to one focused on investment or savings.

Example: A pure term insurance policy is the most common type of protection plan, as its sole purpose is to provide financial protection to the family in case of the breadwinner's death.

Provision

A specific clause, term, or condition within an insurance policy.

Example: The 'Grace Period' is a standard provision in every life insurance policy, granting the policyholder an extra 30 days to pay their premium.

Pure Endowment

A contract that pays a benefit only if the insured is alive on a specific future date. It provides no death benefit if the insured dies before that date.

Example: A parent buys a pure endowment for their child that pays ₹10 lakh if the child reaches age 21. If the child tragically passes away at age 19, no benefit is paid. This product is rare today.

Q

Qualified Plan

A retirement or employee benefit plan that meets specific IRS requirements, allowing for tax-deductible contributions and tax-deferred growth (e.g., 401(k), pension plans).

Example: A company's 401(k) plan is a qualified plan. This means the contributions made by the employer are tax-deductible for the business, and the employee's investments grow tax-deferred until retirement.

Qualifying Life Event

A change in an individual's situation—like marriage, birth of a child, or loss of other health coverage—that allows them to enroll in a health plan outside of the normal open enrollment period.

Example: After getting married, which is a qualifying life event, Maya was able to add her new husband to her company's health insurance plan, even though it was not the regular enrollment time of the year.

Quarterly Premium

A premium payment mode where payments are made once every three months.

Example: A policy with a ₹12,000 annual premium can be paid with a quarterly premium of approximately ₹3,050 every three months.

Quotation

An estimate of the premium for a specific type and amount of insurance coverage for a particular individual.

Example: An agent provides a customer with a quotation showing that a ₹1 crore term plan for a 30-year-old non-smoker would cost around ₹12,000 per year.

R

Rebate

The illegal practice of an agent giving a portion of their commission or some other financial inducement to a client as an incentive to buy a policy.

Example: An agent tells a client, "If you buy this policy from me, I will give you back 10% of the first premium from my commission." This is an illegal rebate.

Reduced Paid-Up Insurance

A non-forfeiture option where the policy's cash value is used as a single premium to purchase a whole life policy with the same terms as the original, but for a lower face amount.

Example: Shreya stops paying premiums on her ₹20 lakh endowment policy. She chooses the reduced paid-up option. Her policy continues with no more premiums, but the sum assured is now reduced to ₹8 lakh.

Reduced Sum Assured

The lower death benefit payable under a policy that has been converted to 'paid-up' status.

Example: A policy's original sum assured was ₹10 lakh. After it was made paid-up, the reduced sum assured was calculated to be ₹4 lakh. This is the new death benefit.

Reinstatement

The process of putting a lapsed policy back in force by paying all overdue premiums with interest and providing new evidence of insurability.

Example: Six months after his policy lapsed, Aman wants to restart it. He undergoes a medical check-up and pays all the missed premiums plus interest to complete the reinstatement process.

Reinsurance

The practice of an insurance company (the ceding company) transferring a portion of its risk to another insurance company (the reinsurer). It's insurance for insurance companies.

Example: To avoid having too much exposure on a single high-value policy, an insurer keeps ₹10 crore of the risk and transfers the remaining ₹40 crore to a reinsurance company.

Reinsurer

An insurance company that accepts risk from another insurance company in a reinsurance agreement.

Example: General Re and Swiss Re are famous global reinsurers that take on risk from primary insurance companies like LIC or New India Assurance.

Renewable Term

A term life policy that can be renewed at the end of the term for another term, without evidence of insurability, but with a higher premium based on the insured's attained age.

Example: A 10-year renewable term policy expires. The policyholder, now 10 years older, can renew for another 10 years by simply paying the new, higher premium without any medical questions.

Replacement

The act of terminating an existing life insurance policy or letting it lapse to purchase a new one. This is highly regulated to protect consumers.

Example: An agent convinces a client to surrender their old policy and use the money to buy a new one, often to generate a new commission. This act is called replacement and can be harmful to the client.

Rescission

The act of voiding or canceling an insurance policy from its inception, as if it never existed. This usually happens during the contestability period due to fraud or material misrepresentation.

Example: When an insurer discovers that an applicant lied about a major health condition, they initiate a rescission of the policy, refund the premiums, and treat the contract as null and void from the start.

Retention Limit

The maximum amount of risk an insurance company is willing to carry on a single life. Any amount above this limit is passed on to a reinsurer.

Example: An insurance company has a retention limit of ₹5 crore. When they issue a policy for ₹8 crore, they keep ₹5 crore of the risk themselves and reinsure the remaining ₹3 crore.

Return of Mortality Charges

A feature or rider in some universal life policies that promises to return some or all of the mortality charges to the cash value, often at specified intervals or at death.

Example: In a ULIP with a return of mortality charges feature, all the deductions made for providing life cover are added back to the fund value at the end of the policy term.

Return of Premium

A type of term life insurance rider or policy where, if the insured outlives the policy term, all the premiums they paid are returned to them, tax-free.

Example: Sam pays ₹20,000 per year for a 30-year "Return of Premium" term plan. He survives the 30 years. The insurance company returns the entire ₹6 lakh he paid in premiums back to him.

Revival

Another term for Reinstatement; the process of restoring a lapsed policy.

Example: The agent helped the client with the paperwork for the revival of his lapsed policy, which included paying the outstanding premiums and submitting a health declaration.

Rider

An optional add-on to an insurance policy that provides supplemental benefits or coverage for an additional cost.

Example: A basic term plan only covers death. By paying an extra premium, a policyholder can add a Critical Illness Rider and an Accidental Death Benefit Rider for more comprehensive protection.

Rider Premium

The specific additional premium charged for adding a rider to a policy.

Example: The base premium for a life policy is ₹10,000. The rider premium for adding an accidental benefit is an additional ₹1,500, making the total premium ₹11,500.

Risk

The uncertainty or chance of a loss occurring.

Example: The primary risk that life insurance covers is the financial loss a family would face due to the premature death of an earning member.

Risk Class

A category that groups individuals with a similar level of risk, used to determine premiums. Common classes include Preferred Plus, Preferred, Standard, and Substandard.

Example: An extremely healthy applicant with a clean family medical history may be placed in the "Preferred Plus" risk class and offered the lowest possible premium rate.

Risk Premium

The portion of the premium needed to pay for expected losses, not including expenses or profit. (Similar to Net Premium).

Example: Out of a total premium of ₹1,000, the risk premium might be ₹700, which is the amount specifically allocated to cover the probability of a claim. The remaining ₹300 covers company expenses and profit.

Risk Selection

The process of evaluating and classifying insurance applicants based on their degree of risk. Another term for underwriting.

Example: The insurance company's risk selection process involves analyzing medical reports, lifestyle habits, and occupation to decide whether to accept, reject, or charge a higher premium to an applicant.

S

Salary Continuation Plan

An agreement by an employer to continue paying an employee's salary (or a portion of it) after their death or disability, often funded with life or disability insurance owned by the employer.

Example: A company sets up a salary continuation plan for its top executive. If the executive dies, the insurance policy pays the company, which then uses the funds to continue paying a portion of the executive's salary to their family for five years.

Secondary Beneficiary

Another term for Contingent Beneficiary; the person or entity who receives the death benefit if the primary beneficiary is no longer living when the insured dies.

Example: A woman names her husband as the primary beneficiary and her sister as the secondary beneficiary. If her husband dies before her, her sister will automatically become the one to receive the policy money.

Settlement Options

The various ways a policy's death benefit or maturity value can be paid out, such as a lump sum, interest only, fixed period, or fixed amount installments.

Example: Upon maturity of her policy, instead of taking the full ₹20 lakh in one go, the policyholder chose a settlement option to receive the money in 10 equal annual installments.

Settlement Value

The total amount paid to a beneficiary, which may include the face amount plus any accrued bonuses or riders, minus any outstanding loans.

Example: A policy had a face amount of ₹10 lakh, accrued bonuses of ₹2 lakh, and an outstanding loan of ₹1 lakh. The final settlement value paid to the nominee was ₹11 lakh.

Simplified Issue

A type of underwriting that involves a short application with a few health questions but no medical exam. It's more thorough than guaranteed issue but less than full underwriting.

Example: To get a simplified issue policy, an applicant just had to answer five questions like "Have you been treated for cancer in the last 5 years?" and was approved without a medical test.

Single Premium

A type of policy where the entire premium is paid in one single lump-sum payment at the beginning of the contract.

Example: A person receives a retirement bonus and uses ₹10 lakh to buy a single premium life insurance policy. They make one payment and are covered for the entire policy term without any further payments.

Special Risk

A risk that cannot be evaluated using standard underwriting procedures, often due to a rare health condition or an extremely hazardous occupation.

Example: An applicant who is a professional astronaut would be considered a special risk, requiring a custom underwriting assessment and premium calculation.

Standard Risk

A person who, according to an insurer's underwriting standards, is considered to have an average or normal level of risk and is eligible for standard premium rates.

Example: A healthy 35-year-old with a desk job and no history of major illness is classified as a standard risk and is charged the normal premium rate.

State Insurance Department

The government agency in each state responsible for regulating the insurance industry, licensing companies and agents, and protecting consumers.

Example: In India, the IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India) functions like a national state insurance department, setting rules for all insurance companies.

Status Clause

A type of war exclusion clause that states the death benefit will not be paid if the insured dies while serving in the military, regardless of the cause of death.

Example: A soldier with a policy containing a status clause dies in a car accident while on leave. The claim is denied because his death occurred while he held the 'status' of an active-duty soldier.

Straight Life

A common type of whole life insurance where premiums are payable for the entire lifetime of the insured.

Example: A straight life policy requires the policyholder to pay a level premium every year for as long as they live. The premium payments only stop when the insured passes away.

Substandard Risk

An individual who is considered a higher-than-average risk due to factors like poor health, a hazardous job, or a dangerous hobby. They are typically charged a higher premium (rated).

Example: A person with well-managed diabetes might be classified as a substandard risk and charged a 25% higher premium than a standard risk applicant.

Suicide Clause / Suicide Exclusion

A policy provision stating that if the insured commits suicide within a specified period from the policy's issue date (usually one or two years), the insurer will not pay the death benefit but will return the premiums paid.

Example: A person buys a policy and dies by suicide 8 months later. Due to the suicide clause, the insurer denies the death benefit claim and instead refunds all the premiums paid to the nominee.

Sum Assured

The guaranteed amount of money that the insurer agrees to pay upon the death of the insured or at policy maturity. This is the base coverage amount.

Example: The policy document clearly states that the Sum Assured is ₹50,00,000. This is the minimum guaranteed amount that will be paid, with bonuses being extra.

Supplementary Benefits

Additional benefits that can be added to a basic insurance policy, usually in the form of riders.

Example: The main policy provided a death benefit, but the policyholder also opted for supplementary benefits like a critical illness rider and a disability income rider.

Surrender

The act of terminating a cash value policy before its maturity date in order to receive the cash surrender value.

Example: Facing a financial crisis, the policyholder decided to surrender his policy to get immediate access to its accumulated cash value of ₹3 lakh.

Surrender Charges

A fee charged to a policyholder when they surrender a policy for its cash value, typically during the first several years of the policy. The charge decreases over time.

Example: A policy has a 10% surrender charge in the third year. If the policyholder surrenders it when the cash value is ₹1,00,000, the company will deduct ₹10,000 as a surrender charge and pay out ₹90,000.

Surrender Value

The amount payable to the policyholder upon voluntary termination of the policy. It is the accumulated cash value minus any surrender charges and outstanding loans.

Example: A policy's cash value is ₹2,00,000, but there is a ₹15,000 surrender charge and a ₹50,000 outstanding loan. The net surrender value payable is ₹1,35,000.

Survivorship Life Insurance

Another name for a Last-to-Die policy, which covers two or more people and pays the death benefit only after the last person dies.

Example: A couple uses survivorship life insurance to create an inheritance for their disabled child. The policy pays out after both parents have passed away, ensuring funds are available to care for the child for the rest of their life.

Systematic Withdrawal

A settlement option or feature that allows the policyholder or beneficiary to receive regular, automated payments from the policy's cash value or proceeds.

Example: A beneficiary sets up a systematic withdrawal plan on a ₹50 lakh death benefit to receive ₹40,000 every month, providing a steady income stream instead of a single lump sum.

T

Table Rating

A method used to calculate premiums for substandard risks. The applicant is assigned a "table" (e.g., Table B or Table 4), which corresponds to a certain percentage increase over the standard premium rate.

Example: Due to a health condition, an applicant is given a "Table 2" rating, which means their premium will be the standard rate plus an additional 50%.

Target Premium

In a universal life policy, this is the premium suggested by the insurer that, if paid consistently, should keep the policy in force for a long duration under the policy's current assumptions.

Example: The insurer calculates a target premium of ₹3,000 per month for a ULIP. While the policyholder can pay more or less, paying the target premium gives a reasonable expectation that the policy will not lapse prematurely.

Tax-Free Death Benefit

A key feature of life insurance, meaning that the death benefit paid to the beneficiary is generally not subject to federal income tax.

Example: A nominee receives a death benefit of ₹1 crore. This entire amount is tax-free in their hands, meaning they do not have to declare it as income or pay any income tax on it.

Term Assurance

The term commonly used in the United Kingdom and other Commonwealth countries for Term Life Insurance.

Example: A financial advisor in London might recommend "level term assurance" to a client, which is the same as what an advisor in the US or India would call "level term insurance."

Term Conversion

The act of exercising the conversion privilege in a term policy to switch it to a permanent policy without providing evidence of insurability.

Example: Nearing the end of his term plan, and wanting lifelong cover, the policyholder initiates a term conversion to a whole life plan offered by the same insurer.

Term Insurance

A type of life insurance that provides coverage for a specific period (the "term"). It pays a death benefit only if the insured dies during that term and typically has no cash value.

Example: A 30-year-old buys a term insurance policy for 30 years. If she passes away anytime before age 60, her family gets the death benefit. If she lives past 60, the policy expires and pays nothing.

Term Rider

An add-on to a permanent life insurance policy that provides additional, temporary term insurance coverage for a specified period.

Example: A person has a ₹20 lakh whole life policy. While their children are young, they add a 20-year term rider of ₹80 lakh to have a total coverage of ₹1 crore during their high-responsibility years.

Termination Date

The date on which the insurance policy coverage officially ends.

Example: For a 15-year term policy issued on March 5, 2010, the termination date is March 5, 2025.

Third Party Administrator (TPA)

A company that provides administrative services, such as claims processing and record-keeping, for self-funded group plans or insurance companies.

Example: When an employee needs to make a health insurance claim, they don't contact the insurance company directly but instead deal with the TPA, whose name and contact number are on their health card.

Total and Permanent Disability (TPD)

A condition of being permanently unable to work due to illness or injury. Many policies have riders that provide benefits, such as waiver of premium or a lump-sum payment, for TPD.

Example: After an accident resulted in the loss of both arms, the insured was declared to have a Total and Permanent Disability, which triggered the TPD benefit in his insurance policy.

Trust

A legal entity created to hold and manage assets on behalf of a beneficiary. Life insurance policies are often owned by or have their benefits paid to a trust for estate planning purposes.

Example: A couple sets up a trust and names it as the beneficiary of their life insurance policy to ensure the funds are managed professionally for their minor children if both parents were to pass away.

Trustee

The person or institution responsible for administering a trust according to its terms.

Example: A bank is appointed as the trustee for a family trust. The bank's responsibility is to invest the insurance proceeds and distribute the money to the children for their education and welfare as specified in the trust document.

Twisting

The illegal practice of inducing a policyholder to replace their existing policy with a new one through misrepresentation or incomplete comparisons, to the detriment of the policyholder.

Example: An agent falsely tells a client their current policy is bad and convinces them to surrender it and buy a new one, just so the agent can earn a fresh commission. This is twisting.

U

Unbundled Life Insurance

A term for policies like Universal Life where the three main components—the savings element, the mortality charge, and the expense charge—are separate and visible to the policyholder.

Example: A universal life policy is unbundled because the annual statement clearly shows how much of the premium went towards the cost of insurance (mortality), how much went to expenses, and how much was added to the savings/investment component.

Underinsured

A state of having some insurance coverage, but not enough to adequately cover the potential financial loss.

Example: A person who earns ₹20 lakh a year but only has a ₹10 lakh life insurance policy is significantly underinsured, as the death benefit would not be enough to replace their income for their family.

Underwriter

The insurance company employee who is responsible for evaluating risks, selecting who to insure, and determining the appropriate premium rates.

Example: After reviewing an applicant's medical reports, the underwriter decided to approve the policy but with a 25% premium loading due to high cholesterol levels.

Underwriting

The comprehensive process an insurer uses to evaluate an applicant's risk and decide whether to issue a policy and on what terms.

Example: The underwriting process for a large policy involved a medical exam, a review of financial documents, and a telephone interview to assess the applicant's overall risk profile.

Unearned Commission

The portion of an agent's commission that must be returned to the insurer if the policy lapses or is canceled within a certain period after being issued.

Example: An agent received a ₹10,000 commission for a policy. The client canceled the policy during the free-look period. The agent has to return the full ₹10,000 to the company as it is now an unearned commission.

Unearned Premium

The portion of a premium that has been paid by the policyholder but covers a future period of coverage. If the policy is canceled, this portion is typically refunded.

Example: A policyholder pays a ₹12,000 annual premium in January but cancels the policy at the end of June. The insurer must refund the unearned premium of ₹6,000, which covers the second half of the year.

Uniform Policy Provisions

A set of standard clauses and provisions that are required by law to be included in all life insurance policies in a given state to protect consumers.

Example: The 'Grace Period' and 'Incontestability Clause' are uniform policy provisions mandated by the regulator, so they are found in every life insurance policy, regardless of the company.

Universal Life Insurance

A type of flexible permanent life insurance that combines a term insurance component with a tax-deferred savings element. Policyholders can adjust their premiums and death benefit as their needs change.

Example: John buys a universal life policy. In years when he has extra income, he pays a higher premium to build cash value faster. In lean years, he pays a lower premium, and the policy uses the existing cash value to cover its costs.

Unpaid Premium

A premium that is past its due date. If it is not paid by the end of the grace period, the policy will lapse.

Example: A notice from the insurer states, "We are yet to receive your unpaid premium of ₹5,000 which was due on July 1st. Please pay before the grace period ends to avoid policy lapse."

Usual, Customary, and Reasonable (UCR) Fees

The standard amount that medical providers in a certain geographic area typically charge for a specific service. Insurers often use UCR to determine how much to reimburse for claims. More common in health insurance.

Example: A hospital charges ₹1.5 lakh for a surgery, but the UCR fee for that surgery in that city is only ₹1.2 lakh. The insurer will only consider ₹1.2 lakh as the eligible expense for calculating the claim payout.

V

Valuation

The process of calculating an insurance company's policy reserves—the liability it must hold to meet its future obligations to policyholders.

Example: At the end of each financial year, an insurance company must perform a valuation of its liabilities to ensure it has enough assets set aside to cover all the promises made to its policyholders.

Valuation Premium

The premium used by an insurer for the purpose of calculating its policy reserves, which may differ from the actual premium charged to the policyholder.

Example: An insurer may use a more conservative valuation premium in its internal calculations for reserves than the competitive gross premium it charges the customer.

Variable Life Insurance

A type of permanent life insurance where the policy's cash value is invested in a number of sub-accounts (similar to mutual funds). The death benefit and cash value can fluctuate based on the performance of these investments. The policyholder bears the investment risk.

Example: A policyholder allocates her premium in a variable life policy to a 'Blue Chip Equity Fund' sub-account. If the fund performs well, her cash value and death benefit increase. If it performs poorly, they decrease.

Variable Universal Life (VUL)

A policy that combines the flexible premiums and death benefits of universal life with the investment sub-accounts of variable life.

Example: A VUL policy allows a policyholder to choose how much premium to pay each year (flexibility of Universal Life) and also which stock or bond funds to invest that premium in (investment choice of Variable Life).

Vesting

The process of gaining full ownership rights to a benefit, typically in the context of employer-sponsored retirement plans. An employee becomes "fully vested" when they are entitled to 100% of the employer's contributions.

Example: A company has a 3-year vesting period for its pension plan. An employee who leaves after 2 years forfeits the employer's contribution. An employee who leaves after 3 years is fully vested and can take the entire amount, including the employer's contribution.

Voluntary Life Insurance

Optional life insurance coverage offered through an employer where the employee pays the full premium. It's a common part of a group benefits package.

Example: An employer provides a basic ₹5 lakh cover for free. They also offer voluntary life insurance, allowing employees to buy up to ₹50 lakh of additional coverage at a group rate by having the premium deducted from their salary.

W

Waiting Period

A period of time that must pass before an individual is eligible for coverage or benefits. For example, a new employee may have a 90-day waiting period before their group life insurance becomes active. (Similar to Elimination Period for disability).

Example: A new employee must complete a 3-month waiting period before they are eligible to be enrolled in the company's group health and life insurance plans.

Waiting Period Rider

A rider that specifies the duration of the waiting period for certain benefits, like those from a disability income rider.

Example: A critical illness rider has a 90-day waiting period rider, meaning it will not pay any benefit for an illness diagnosed within the first 90 days of the policy's start date.

Waiver of Deductible

A provision in some policies that waives the deductible if the loss is particularly severe. More common in property insurance.

Example: A health policy has a waiver of deductible for major accidents. If the policyholder has a small bill, they pay the deductible. If they are in a severe car crash with a huge bill, the company waives the deductible and pays from the first rupee.

Waiver of Premium

A common rider that waives the policyholder's obligation to pay premiums if they become totally disabled. The policy remains in force as if the premiums were being paid.

Example: After a disabling illness, a policyholder activates the waiver of premium rider. They no longer have to pay their annual premium of ₹25,000, but their life insurance policy continues to be active and grow.

Whole Life Insurance

The original form of permanent life insurance. It provides lifelong coverage with a guaranteed death benefit, level premiums, and a cash value that grows at a guaranteed rate.

Example: A person buys a whole life insurance policy at age 30. They pay a fixed premium every year. The policy provides a death benefit no matter when they die, be it at age 50 or 90, and it builds a guaranteed cash value over time.

Written Premium

The total amount of premiums for all policies that an insurer has issued (or "written") during a specific period of time.

Example: In the first quarter, an insurance company sold 1,000 policies with an average premium of ₹10,000 each. The company's written premium for that quarter is ₹1 crore.

Z

Zero-Coupon Life Insurance

A conceptual term, not a standard product. It could refer to a single-premium policy where the growth acts like a zero-coupon bond, maturing to a certain face amount in the future.

Example: A person might pay ₹5 lakh today for a "zero-coupon" type of policy that guarantees a payout of ₹20 lakh in 20 years, with no other payments or features in between.
